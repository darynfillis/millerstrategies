Federal policy issues can move quickly. These questions are designed for executives, general counsel, government affairs leaders, and organizations trying to understand whether Washington engagement is needed and where to begin.

Working with Miller Strategies

What does Miller Strategies do?

Miller Strategies helps companies and organizations navigate federal advocacy, legislative strategy, regulatory risk, policy intelligence, coalition building, and Washington decision-making.

When should a company contact Miller Strategies?

A company should contact Miller Strategies when a federal policy, agency action, legislative issue, investigation, regulatory decision, or political development could materially affect the business and leadership needs a clear path forward.

What makes Miller Strategies different from a traditional lobbying firm?

Miller Strategies focuses on judgment, timing, access, and execution. The work starts with the client’s risk and the real decision path, not with generic activity or broad outreach.

Does Miller Strategies only work with large companies?

Miller Strategies works with organizations facing consequential federal policy issues. The relevant question is not only company size, but whether the issue is significant enough to require senior-level strategy, access, and disciplined execution.

Federal Advocacy and Legislative Strategy

What is federal advocacy?

Federal advocacy is the process of helping policymakers, agencies, congressional offices, and relevant stakeholders understand how a federal decision could affect a company, industry, community, or public-policy objective.

How does Miller Strategies approach legislative advocacy?

The firm identifies the offices, committees, staff, leadership channels, stakeholders, and timing that matter. From there, it helps shape a message that is credible, useful, and relevant to the decision-makers involved.

Can Miller Strategies help when a bill is already moving?

Yes, but timing matters. If the legislative window is still open, the firm can help assess where influence still exists, which offices matter, and what type of engagement could change the outcome.

What should executives know before a Hill meeting?

Executives should understand the policy issue, the relevant offices, the stakeholder landscape, the likely objections, the desired outcome, and the specific reason the meeting matters to that office.

Regulatory Strategy and Risk

How can Miller Strategies help with regulatory risk?

Miller Strategies helps clients understand whether the risk is agency-driven, legislative, executive branch, political, or a combination. The firm then helps leadership identify practical options while the issue is still influenceable.

What is regulatory strategy?

Regulatory strategy is the process of understanding how agency action, enforcement posture, rulemaking, guidance, or administrative priorities could affect a business and developing a plan to engage before the result is locked in.

Can Miller Strategies help translate technical business issues for policymakers?

Yes. Many companies have strong technical arguments that do not immediately translate into policy relevance. Miller Strategies helps convert those issues into clear, credible arguments that policymakers can understand.

What industries does Miller Strategies understand?

The firm has experience across technology, financial services, energy, healthcare, transportation, defense, national security, agriculture, consumer products, and other regulated or policy-sensitive sectors.

Policy Intelligence and Scenario Planning

What is policy intelligence?

Policy intelligence is practical insight into what is moving in Washington, who is involved, what decisions are still open, and how those developments may affect a client’s business or policy objective.

How does policy intelligence help executives?

Policy intelligence helps executives separate political noise from actionable risk. It gives leadership a clearer understanding of what is real, what may happen next, and where engagement can still matter.

What is scenario planning in government affairs?

Scenario planning identifies likely policy paths, timing, stakeholders, risks, and decision points so a client can prepare for multiple outcomes rather than react after the fact.

Can Miller Strategies prepare executive briefings?

Yes. The firm prepares concise, decision-ready materials for executives, general counsel, government affairs leaders, board discussions, Hill meetings, agency engagement, and principal-level conversations.

Coalitions, Stakeholders, and Public-Private Engagement

Why does coalition building matter?

Policymakers often give more weight to an issue when it is supported by credible voices beyond one company. Coalitions can show broader public-policy, economic, community, or industry relevance.

How does Miller Strategies identify stakeholders?

The firm maps supporters, opposition, validators, trade associations, outside groups, staff, agency contacts, member offices, and other players that may influence the decision path.

What is public-private engagement strategy?

Public-private engagement strategy creates credible channels between industry leaders and policymakers before decisions are locked in. The goal is to make the client a useful resource, not just a late-stage objector.

Can Miller Strategies help with message discipline?

Yes. The firm helps clients align the message with the audience, timing, decision path, and specific policy objective.

Engagement, Confidentiality, and Next Steps

Is an initial conversation confidential?

Miller Strategies treats initial conversations with discretion. A formal client relationship, including any specific confidentiality obligations, is established through an executed written agreement.

What should a company prepare before contacting Miller Strategies?

A company should be ready to explain the issue, what is at risk, what decision or government action is pending, which stakeholders are involved, and the timeline for action.

Does contacting Miller Strategies create a client relationship?

No. Contacting the firm through the website, phone, email, or an introductory conversation does not create a client, legal, lobbying, advisory, fiduciary, or confidential relationship unless and until a written agreement is executed.
